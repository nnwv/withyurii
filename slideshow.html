Pictures help us care for the world. It is through them through them that we experienced our “togetherness.”

3

Most of us have been staring at the blue screens of computers and related electronic devices for years, without giving them much thought. We relate to them the way a medieval church-goer related to the wall on which a religious scene had been painted, or as the camera obscura's user related to the images through which he viewed an eclipse of the sun: as the surface on which other things are displayed. We look through, not at them.

5

We are also now spending much of our lives inside this space, much as our medieval predecessors spent theirs inside the Great Chain of Being. However, we never feel as if we are together there. How could we, since there is no “there”—no place, just amorphous space.

6

With my digital gallery, I want to return to the moment in which the sky became blue...

7

...and the world was seen in the way that it wanted to be seen: as a picture, rather than a representation.

8

Some of the pictures that I will hang on the walls of this gallery will have been drawn with the pencil of nature,

9

Others will be co-created—collaborations between the world and us.

10

But in both cases, they will ”welcome difference not as damage, but potential” and model what Helen Chadwick called a “vigorous plurality of interactions.”
